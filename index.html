Bio

I am a doctoral candidate working with the Brain Dynamics and Control Research Group in the Preston M. Green Department of Electrical and Systems Engineering at Washington University in St. Louis. I am mentored by Dr. ShiNung Ching. The aim of my research is to understand the dynamics of neural coding with specific focus on olfactory coding.

To develop this models, I use a broad range of computational methods spanning from classical control theory to machine learning. Interestingly, my research has shown that some of these rigorous mathematical models are remarkably in-sync with their biological counterparts. Moreover, they have lent interpretability to some existing data-driven computational models.

Prior to this, I completed Bachelor of Engineering from Jadavpur University, Kolkata in 2015. In 2018, I obtained the Master of Science degree in Electrical Engineering from Washington University in St. Louis. I am passionate about learning new concepts and techniques that will help us understand and emulate the highly optimized biological functions.

Research Projects

Normative modeling of neural circuit dynamics to meet control & detection objectives

Our perception of smell is difficult to characterize. Olfactory stimuli mostly arrive in the form of invisible molecules suspended in a fluid medium. Often, we rely on our other sensory capabilities to help us identify and characterize the stimulus. For instance, we identify immediately the smell of an orange if we can see it. If blindfolded? Not so easily! However, organisms with less evolved cognitive abilities such as the fruit fly navigate in their environment relying on the sense of smell. My primary research question was, can we characterize this act of smelling mathematically? Given that we successfully build a computational model of olfaction, will it be predictive of neural architectures known to exist in organisms? Finally, can these models depict properties that are often associated by researchers with olfaction, but are not an integral part of the modeling efforts? To answer this questions we hypothesized that early olfactory networks convert the complex high-dimensional chemical signals into corresponding low-dimensional, usable formats. Based on this informed hypothesis, we devised an optimization problem. We found that the solution was remarkably similar to electrophysiological recordings made by our collaborators. On further investigation, we found that this framework allows us to delve into the mechanisms underlying neural computation. This predictive setup laid the foundation for an in-depth analysis of the capabilities of the olfactory system.

Modeling neural and behavioral adaptation over multiple timescales

A sensory signal is first converted to a neural code by the receptors and the early sensory neurons. This neural code after a sequence of systematic transformations become behavior. One interesting observation made by our collaborators was: when a stimulus is repeated, neural activity in C. elegans adapts rapidly. However, the behavior corresponding to the signal continues for much longer in these organisms. In this piece of work, we sought to investigate if we can mathematically model this phenomenon building on our top-down model of sensory detection. Moreover, can the model characterize qualitatively and quantitatively how the different parameters associated with a stimulus (intensity, frequency etc.) influences neural activity and behavior.

Using Theory of Optimal Control as a framework for understanding neural dynamics

Theory of optimal control has been widely investigated in the context of a wide range of scientific and engineering problems. It is often applied to understand and emulate biological movements. In my research, we explore a number of problems in the context of sensory neural coding. If sensory neurons in fact exploit the information contained in peripheral stimuli and generate low-dimensional representations, then we can conceive that sensory neurons essentially 'control' downstream high-level processes. Additionally, we can quantify the quality of the sensory neural code via a performance criteria. Integrating these concepts, we have outlined the definition of 'optimal control theory' as a tool to study sensory neural coding. In this framework, now we can characterize neural dynamics generated during a variety of sensory tasks from a normative standpoint.
