Bio

I am a doctoral candidate working with the Brain Dynamics and Control Research Group in the Preston M. Green Department of Electrical and Systems Engineering at Washington University in St. Louis. I am mentored by Dr. ShiNung Ching. The aim of my research is to understand the dynamics of neural coding with specific focus on olfactory coding.

To develop this models, I use a broad range of computational methods spanning from classical control theory to machine learning. Interestingly, my research has shown that some of these rigorous mathematical models are remarkably in-sync with their biological counterparts. Moreover, they have lent interpretability to some existing data-driven computational models.

Prior to this, I completed Bachelor of Engineering from Jadavpur University, Kolkata in 2015. In 2018, I obtained the Master of Science degree in Electrical Engineering from Washington University in St. Louis. I am passionate about learning new concepts and techniques that will help us understand and emulate the highly optimized biological functions.

Normative modeling of neural circuit dynamics to meet control & detection objectives

Our perception of smell is difficult to characterize. Olfactory stimuli mostly arrive in the form of invisible molecules suspended in a fluid medium. Often, we rely on our other sensory capabilities to help us identify and characterize the stimulus. For instance, we identify immediately the smell of an orange if we can see it. If blindfolded? Not so easily! However, organisms with less evolved cognitive abilities such as the fruit fly navigate in their environment relying on the sense of smell.

My primary research question was, can we characterize this act of smelling mathematically? Given that we successfully build a computational model of olfaction, will it be predictive of neural architectures known to exist in organisms? Finally, can these models depict properties that are often associated by researchers with olfaction, but are not an integral part of the modeling efforts?

To answer this questions I hypothesized that early olfactory networks convert the complex high-dimensional chemical signals into corresponding low-dimensional, usable formats. Based on this informed hypothesis, I devised an optimization problem. I found that the solution was remarkably similar to electrophysiological recordings made by our collaborators. On further investigation, I found that this framework allows us to delve into the mechanisms underlying neural computation. This predictive setup laid the foundation for an in-depth analysis of the capabilities of the olfactory system.

Concepts: Computional Neuroscience, Control Theory, Optimization

Normative modeling of neural and behavioral adaptation over multiple timescales

Did you ever venture into perfume shopping and midway through the process found yourself unable to identify any odor? Have you relied on a pot of coffee beans to give you a better judgement of a set of fragrance? This temporary inability to perceive odors is considered to be a phenomenon known as habituation . What happens here is, after repeatedly encountering the same odor several times, the neural activity adapts to a lower magnitude response, hence causing the desensitization.

In my research, I investigated if building on the framework of sensory detection, this interesting response feature can be realized. Furthermore, if doing that would give us a glimpse of the mechanisms behind such response characteristic. My model showed that processing of sensory neural code over multiple timescales: (a) an instantaneous one used for immediate detection, and, (b) a slow one that encodes information over a past horizon leads to adapting neural response.

My collaborators at Worcester Polytechnic Institute around the same time made an interesting observation. They found through experimentation in C. elegans while the neural response showed drastic adaptation, behavior adapted much slowly. In fact for some stimulus concentrations and repeat frequencies, behavior remains invariant for a few hours. I used my developed neural model to steer a probabilistic decoder, which at each timestep computes how likely it is to execute a behavior. I found that for a finite set of behavior, indeed the behavioral traits do not linearly follow the magnitude of neural response. We are currently compiling our predictions and observations for this project and hope to have it out soon!

Concepts: Computional Neuroscience, Control Theory, Optimization, Machine Learning

Tools: MATLAB

Using Theory of Optimal Control as a framework for understanding neural dynamics

Theory of optimal control has been widely investigated in the context of a wide range of scientific and engineering problems. Several research groups have applied this paradigm to understand and emulate biological movements. But can we use methods motivated by optimal control to understand sensory functionalities? It turns out, we can. If we consider that activity of sensory neurons control downstream high-level processes, then we can search for the best strategy for control. Additionally, we can summarize the performance of this dynamical system using a mathematical criteria. Taken together, this essentially outlines the central theme of optimal control theory. The optimal solution as well as the solution strategies give us clarity about sophisticated biological functions and how heterogenous populations of neurons achieve those through synaptic interactions. Our problem setup has led us to interesting observations about excitatory-inhibitory balance in neural networks, feedback from higher brain regions during learning etc. I am presenting this work at Bernstein Conference 2020 and hope to have an article out for peer review soon.

Concepts: Computional Neuroscience, Control Theory, Optimization

Tools: MATLAB

Epileptic Seizure Recognition

We implemented Kernel-based Soft Margin Support Vector Machine to distinguish between healthy and epileptic EEG signals. For this project, we used the open source Epileptic Seizure Recognition dataset made available by University of Bonn and preprocessed by University of California, Irvine. We looked at 1s long (178 data-points/features) EEG traces recorded from different brain regions in 5 subjects. We found that the information embedded in these short recordings could be faithfully represented through a 75-dimensional feature space. The developed analysis framework was able to distinguish between not only healthy and seizure EEG traces from epiletic region of the brain, but also between recordings from different brain regions and under different recording techniques.

Concepts: Machine Learning, Time Series Analysis, Dimensionality Reduction

Tools: MATLAB

Functional Bayesian Analysis of fMRI data

We analyzed fMRI data obtained from 133 subjects (122 children, 33 adults) while they were watching a short, animated movie with no audio using Bayesian methods. The aim of this project was to identify if individual differences in processing cognitive stimuli can be clustered into age groups. Functional data are often characterized using a basis set of smooth time-varying functions. In the first step, we did that via Bayesian Functional Pincipal Component Analysis. Thereafter, we proposed two methods for obtaining the posterior distribution over weights (these weights determine the relative importance of each smooth function). After 10000 iterations of Gibb's sampling using the simpler model, we started observing specific patterns for the posterior distribution for each candidate.

Concepts: Bayesian Machine Learning, Functional Data Analysis, Dimensionality Reduction

Tools: MATLAB

Undergraduate Research

Facial Recognition under limited resources.

In this project, we implemented our algorithm for face identification under constraints of modest computational resources. Face identification in general is a two step process: face localization and face verification. To perform this task, we first created a custom database that contained a set of images for each subject accounting for effects of scaling and rotation. Our algorithm worked by iteratively computing scaled and rotated versions of the template image and finding the region with maximum cross correlation coefficient with the modified template. To improve algorithm performance (i.e., reduce Type I and Type II errors) produce by the baseline method, we extracted features of input and template images using pre-existing algorithms and parsed the features through the pipeline.

Concepts: Computer Vision, Signal Processing
